# Survival Analysis of Time-to-Exploitation

How long does an edge-appliance CVE "survive" before exploitation is confirmed?

**115 edge CVEs** across **13 vendors**, analyzed as survival subjects.
Overall Kaplan-Meier median survival: **36 days**. By 30 days post-disclosure,
**48.7%** of edge CVEs are already confirmed-exploited; by one year, **67.0%**.

Generated by [`scripts/analyze_survival.py`](../scripts/analyze_survival.py)
(Python standard library only -- the Kaplan-Meier estimator is implemented from
first principles, no `lifelines` / `scipy` / `numpy`).

> This appears to be the first application of survival analysis to
> time-to-exploitation at the *vendor* level for a curated edge-appliance KEV
> corpus. The companion [TIME-TO-EXPLOIT.md](TIME-TO-EXPLOIT.md) reports the
> raw TTE distribution; this document models the same quantity as a survival
> process so the *shape* of attacker speed -- not just a point estimate --
> becomes legible.

## Why survival analysis?

Time-to-exploitation (TTE) is the canonical *time-to-event* quantity:

- The **subject** is a CVE, observed from its NVD publication date.
- The **clock** runs in days post-disclosure.
- The **event** is *exploitation confirmed* -- operationalized as the CVE
  appearing in the CISA Known Exploited Vulnerabilities (KEV) catalog.

A raw mean or median of TTE throws away the structure of the process. Two
problems in particular motivate a survival model:

1. **Heavy right skew.** The TTE distribution has a short body and a very long
   tail: most edge CVEs are KEV-listed within weeks, but a minority of legacy
   vulnerabilities (disclosed years before KEV existed in Nov 2021) were swept
   in retroactively. In this corpus the **mean TTE is 375 days** while the
   **median is 36 days** -- a 10x gap driven entirely by that tail. The mean is
   not a meaningful description of attacker speed; it is an artifact of the
   tail. A point median fixes the location but still tells you nothing about
   the curve.

2. **Horizon questions are what defenders actually ask.** "What fraction of
   edge CVEs are exploited within 7 days? 30 days? 90 days?" These are
   statements about the *survival function* `S(t)`, not about any single
   summary statistic. Kaplan-Meier estimates the whole `S(t)` curve
   non-parametrically and reads each horizon directly off it.

The **Kaplan-Meier (product-limit) estimator** is the standard
non-parametric tool here. It makes no distributional assumption (no implicit
Gaussian, no exponential hazard), reads the median robustly off `S(t) = 0.5`,
and -- critically -- is the correct framework the moment any subject becomes
*right-censored* (see Limitations). Even though this corpus has no censoring
today, building the estimate as a survival curve makes the analysis honest and
extensible rather than locking in a fragile point estimate.

## Method

### Survival time

For each CVE:

```
t = max(0, KEV dateAdded - NVD publishedDate)   # in days
```

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| NVD `publishedDate` | Public-disclosure proxy: first appearance in the NVD |
| KEV `dateAdded` | When CISA confirmed active exploitation in the wild |
| **`t = 0`** | Exploited at or before disclosure -- a true zero-day. Negative TTE (KEV listed *before* NVD publication) is folded to `t = 0`, since the appliance was already under attack at the moment of public disclosure. |

**28 of 115** CVEs have `TTE <= 0` and are placed at `t = 0`.

### The event and the estimator

Every CVE in this corpus is *eventually* KEV-listed, so every subject
experiences the event -- there is no censoring in the current data. The
Kaplan-Meier survival function is the product-limit estimate:

```
S(t) = PROD over event times t_i <= t of ( 1 - d_i / n_i )

  n_i = number still "at risk" (not yet KEV-listed) just before t_i
  d_i = number of CVEs KEV-listed exactly at t_i
```

`S(t)` reads as **P(a CVE is not-yet-KEV-listed at `t` days post-disclosure)**,
and `1 - S(t)` is the **cumulative confirmed-exploitation rate** by day `t`.
The KM **median survival** is the smallest `t` at which `S(t) <= 0.5`.

With no censoring the product-limit estimate reduces to the empirical
complement of the CDF, `S(t) = (subjects with time > t) / n`; the script
implements and reports the full product-limit form (at-risk and event counts at
each step) so the method generalizes to a censored future corpus unchanged.

### Stratification

The same curve is computed for the whole corpus, then stratified two ways:

- **By vendor** (13 strata), to compare attacker prioritization across the
  edge ecosystem.
- **By CVSS v3.1 severity band** (CRITICAL >= 9.0, HIGH 7.0-8.9,
  MEDIUM 4.0-6.9, LOW 0.1-3.9), to test whether nominal severity predicts
  exploitation speed.

## Results

### Overall Kaplan-Meier

| Metric | Value |
|--------|------:|
| Subjects | 115 |
| **KM median survival** | **36 days** |
| Raw median TTE | 36 days |
| Raw mean TTE | 375.2 days |
| Zero-days folded to `t = 0` | 28 |

The KM median (36 days) coincides with the raw median, as expected with no
censoring -- but the curve below shows what the point estimate hides.

### Survival at key horizons

`S(t)` = fraction *not yet* confirmed-exploited at `t` days post-disclosure;
`1 - S(t)` = cumulative confirmed-exploitation rate.

| Horizon | S(t) | Exploited by t |
|--------:|-----:|---------------:|
| 0 days | 75.7% | **24.3%** |
| 7 days | 59.1% | **40.9%** |
| 30 days | 51.3% | **48.7%** |
| 90 days | 41.7% | **58.3%** |
| 365 days | 33.0% | **67.0%** |

The single most striking number: **24.3% of edge CVEs are confirmed-exploited
at or before public disclosure** (`t = 0`). The curve then drops fast through
the first week (down to 59.1%) and crosses the median at day 36 -- so roughly
**two in five** edge CVEs are exploited within a week, and **half** within
about five weeks. The long, slow tail past 90 days (still 41.7% "surviving")
is the legacy-CVE population that inflates the mean.

The vendor split is bimodal. **Fast-exploitation vendors** -- Fortinet,
Citrix, Palo Alto, F5, Array Networks, Check Point -- have KM medians inside
**8 days**: their KEV-listed edge CVEs are overwhelmingly fresh, attacker-
prioritized zero-days and near-zero-days. **Slow-tail vendors** -- Sophos
(546d), Cisco (378d), SonicWall (236d), Ivanti (160d) -- have medians dominated
by older CVEs that entered KEV well after disclosure.

Two cautions on the small strata: Check Point (n=2) and Array Networks (n=2)
medians are individual data points, not stable estimates. And a "slow" median
does not mean a vendor is safe -- Ivanti survives at 62% through 90 days but
then collapses to 31% by one year, the signature of a vendor whose older
appliances are repeatedly re-targeted.

Severity does order exploitation speed in the expected direction:
**CRITICAL CVEs survive shortest** (median 17 days, only 39% surviving at 90
days) and HIGH/MEDIUM survive progressively longer. CVSS band is a coarse but
real signal of *how fast* an edge CVE will be exploited, not just *whether*.
(LOW n=1 and UNKNOWN n=4 are too small to interpret.)

## Reproducing

```bash
# Text summary (overall + per-vendor + per-band KM)
python3 scripts/analyze_survival.py --no-fetch

# Markdown tables (as embedded above)
python3 scripts/analyze_survival.py --no-fetch --format markdown

# Machine-readable curve (full step table, horizons, strata)
python3 scripts/analyze_survival.py --no-fetch --format json
```

Omit `--no-fetch` to merge the *live* CISA KEV `dateAdded` instead of the
snapshot baked into `kev_edge_enriched.json`. Both paths are deterministic
given their inputs; `--no-fetch` reproduces the figures in this document
exactly.

## Limitations

This is an honest model of a *confirmation-latency* process, not of true
exploitation onset. The following caveats are load-bearing:

1. **KEV `dateAdded` is a confirmation-latency proxy, not exploitation onset.**
   The event we observe is *CISA confirmed and catalogued* exploitation, which
   lags actual first-exploitation by an unknown, variable amount (hours for a
   loud mass-scanning campaign; weeks-to-months for a quiet APT). Every `S(t)`
   here is therefore a **conservative upper bound on survival** -- real
   exploitation curves drop *faster* than these. The 24.3% at `t = 0` is a
   floor on the true zero-day rate, not a ceiling.

2. **No true right-censoring.** Classical survival analysis derives its power
   from handling subjects who have *not yet* experienced the event at the end
   of observation. This corpus is, by construction, the set of CVEs that *were*
   KEV-listed, so every subject is an event and `S(t)` reaches 0. There is no
   "still surviving at study end" population here. Consequently the KM estimate
   coincides with the empirical complementary CDF; the product-limit machinery
   is correct but its censoring-handling advantage is unused. **The unbiased
   way to introduce censoring would be to widen the cohort to all *disclosed*
   edge CVEs** (KEV-listed or not) and treat the never-listed ones as censored
   at the snapshot date -- a meaningfully different and larger study left to
   future work.

3. **Survivorship / selection bias in the cohort.** Because membership is
   conditioned on eventual KEV listing, fast-and-loud exploitation is
   over-represented relative to vulnerabilities that are exploited quietly and
   never catalogued. The curve describes *catalogued* edge exploitation, which
   is the defender-relevant population but not the universe of all exploitation.

4. **NVD publish-date noise.** `publishedDate` can lag vendor advisories by
   days, and a handful of legacy CVEs were published years after the
   vulnerability was known. This adds noise to short-horizon `t` and is the
   primary driver of the negative-TTE-folded-to-zero population.

5. **Small strata.** Several vendor strata (Check Point n=2, Array Networks
   n=2) and CVSS bands (LOW n=1) are too small for stable KM estimates; their
   medians are single observations and should be read as anecdotes, not rates.

6. **No confidence intervals.** The script reports point estimates of `S(t)`.
   Greenwood-variance confidence bands and a log-rank test for the vendor /
   CVSS stratum differences are the natural next additions (both implementable
   in pure stdlib) and are noted in the project ROADMAP.
