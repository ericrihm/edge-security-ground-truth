A CV of 0.47 indicates moderate dispersion — vendors differ, but not dramatically. The range spans 2 (Check Point) to 18 (Fortinet).

## Concentration Metrics

| Metric | Value | Interpretation |
|--------|------:|----------------|
| HHI | 0.1089 | 1.20x equal-share baseline (0.0909) |
| Gini | 0.246 | Moderate inequality |

The Herfindahl-Hirschman Index at 1.20x the equal baseline shows the distribution is **near-uniform** — no single vendor dominates. For comparison, a truly concentrated distribution (one vendor with 50%+) would yield HHI > 0.25.

The Gini coefficient of 0.246 confirms moderate inequality. This is lower than most real-world distributions, meaning exploited CVEs are spread relatively evenly across vendors.

## Chi-Squared Uniformity Test

**H₀:** All vendors have equal exploitation rates.

- χ²(10) = 21.20
- p ≈ 0.0198

**Result:** REJECT H₀ (p < 0.05). Vendor counts differ significantly from a uniform distribution.

> **Critical caveat:** Rejecting uniformity does NOT demonstrate that vendors differ in code quality. The primary confounders — installed base, researcher attention, and disclosure transparency — are uncontrolled. This test confirms the *distribution is uneven*, not *why*.

Fortinet's 18 CVEs vs. Check Point's 2 could reflect: (a) more devices deployed and therefore more targets, (b) more security researchers examining Fortinet products, (c) Fortinet's broader product surface area, or (d) actual differences in development practices. These factors cannot be separated with KEV data alone.

## EPSS-Count Correlation

**Question:** Do vendors with more CVEs also have higher-severity CVEs (as measured by EPSS)?

- Spearman ρ = -0.175 (n=9, p ≈ 0.638)
- **Interpretation: Not significant**

There is no correlation between a vendor's CVE count and their mean EPSS score. Having more exploited CVEs does not predict whether those CVEs are individually more dangerous. This means raw counts alone are an incomplete risk metric.

## Interpretation Guide

1. **Statistical significance ≠ practical significance.** Even when counts differ significantly, the confounders (installed base, researcher focus, disclosure culture) are uncontrolled. Use these statistics to frame questions, not to rank vendors.

2. **The Gini coefficient measures inequality in the distribution, not quality.** A Gini near 0 means vendors are equally exploited — not equally secure. A vendor could have few CVEs because their product has low market share, not because it's better engineered.

3. **EPSS-count correlation tests volume vs. severity.** The null result here is informative: more CVEs ≠ worse CVEs. Some vendors accumulate many low-severity CVEs; others have fewer but more critical ones.

4. **Ransomware association is the highest-signal metric for defenders.** CISA's ransomware flag is binary and conservative — a CVE only gets flagged when there is confirmed ransomware campaign use. The 43% rate for edge devices far exceeds the overall KEV rate.

## Methodology

All statistical computations use Python stdlib only (no numpy/scipy). The chi-squared p-value uses the Wilson-Hilferty normal approximation. Spearman rank correlation uses a t-distribution approximation for significance. These are adequate for n=11 vendors; results should not be over-interpreted for such small samples.
